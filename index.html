Introduction

Our project is on airplane crashes and fatalities since 1908. We envisioned the user of this dataset as someone planning a trip who wants to make informed decisions about their potential safety, with their questions falling into four main categories: Where have the most crashes happened? When do the most crashes happen? What are the most common causes of accidents? And finally, if I'm in a crash, how likely am I to survive?

Key Findings

From looking at the map above, we can see that crashes happen everywhere, and that they've gotten more common over time. Don't let that be a cause for alarm, though: as air travel increases and there are more planes in the sky, it's not surprising that there would also be more crashes. As expected, the number of fatalities in crashes was initially fairly low; crashes with high numbers of fatalities didn't start occurring until aircraft capacity increased in the 1950s and 1960s.
Perhaps most relevant to modern-day travel, looking at only the past decade of crashes we see that although the number of crashes is fairly evenly distributed among the continents, the highest concentrations of fatalities generally occur around the Mediterranean and in southeast Asia.

Causes

What are the most common causes of accidents?

In the right side of the chart there are the different categories that enclose the similar crash causes. The order of these categories has been sorted from most to least frequent, meaning that the categories on top have caused more crashes than the categories on the bottom. In the center of the chart, there are the actual observations, which in this case represent the crashes. The crashes have been divided according to their specific operator. Each operator has been given a distinct color: commercial, military, and private. Additionally, through digital interaction, the user is able to observe the distribution of a particular crash cause over time. This allows to pick up for any particular trends over the years.

On the right side there are two key legends. The first regards the color encoding of the operators, which is indeed trivial and intuitive to understand. The second legend is a bit more involved, and of extreme importance for the user to be able to understand. It is a note about the scale of the data. In order to represent the data in a clear way, and at the same time preserve the structure and overall patterns within the data, it was necessary to rescale to number of observations. This was necessary given that the high numbers in frequency of certain categories made it difficult for each individual circle in the graph to be clearly displayed. As a result, a scaling mechanism in which one circle in the visualization represents 16 actual crashes in the dataset made it possible to correct for the unclarity that was due to the voluminous number of observations. Hence, with this scaling mechanism, the user is provided with a less populated visualization which results in clarity, and at the same time still benefit of the patterns within the data.

Visualizations and Key Findings

  • The top 5 categories that contain most of the crash causes are: weather, general failure, equipment malfunction, collision, and pilot negligence.
  • Upon further inspection of the yearly distributions of the crash causes over the years, a decline incause frequency is noticeable. Such decline could be explained by progress in technology & other advancements which have made flights safer.
